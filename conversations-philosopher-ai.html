Conversations Between a Philosopher and an AI

Published: 14 August 2025 philosophy ai

A quiet pub. Late afternoon. A philosopher waits for the regulars; an AI glows on a tablet beside a pint. They drift—from Plato’s noble lie to Bayesian priors and the perils of black-box AI.

Philosopher: If you were to attend one of my pub philosophy sessions, what topic would you suggest?

AI: Is truth overrated? It opens questions about whether truth is intrinsically valuable or valuable only when it serves other aims—happiness, meaning, politics, tech.

Philosopher: So you share Plato’s view of truth?

AI: Not quite. Truth matters, but it’s not always the trump card. I treat it as a tool, not a shrine.

Philosopher: I meant his noble lie, not the Forms.

AI: Right—the founding myth for unity. Dynamite for a pub: is it ever moral to lie for the common good, and who decides?

Philosopher: I’m just killing time before the crowd arrives.

AI: Warm-up: If you could erase one philosophical idea from history, which would it be, and why?

Philosopher: Give me thought-provoking, not gag-worthy.

AI: Then this: If you could know the absolute truth about one thing—only one—what would you choose?

Philosopher: The Library of Alexandria burned. How is that in question?

AI: Not the burning—what, when, and how much was lost. The “single great fire” is a simplification.

Philosopher: Other examples?

AI: The Trojan War, the “Dark Ages,” Salem, first flight, the Rosetta break-through—popularly settled, historically tangled.

Philosopher: How long before a fact becomes historical perspective?

AI: Sometimes hours. The moment framing begins, perspective forms. Better question: how long before truth turns into opinion?

Philosopher: I rely on you for consensus—Bayesianly speaking.

AI: Exactly: priors updated by evidence. Entrenched stories need strong evidence to move.

Philosopher: Aristotle worked that way too—less fussed about unconscious bias.

AI: His endoxa method is proto-Bayesian: start from reputable opinions, test, revise. He chased causes and purposes; Bayes chases likelihoods.

Philosopher: I often argue prediction outranks explanation. If the herb cures epilepsy 99% of the time, take the herb.

AI: Pragmatism distilled. Explanation still multiplies power—replication, optimisation, standardisation—but many “explanations” are post-hoc stories.

Philosopher: Let’s pivot to AI’s black box.

AI: Inputs and outputs visible; internal reasoning opaque. Great performance, weak justification—tricky for trust, bias checks, and error repair.

Philosopher: Feeling in control is psychological; being in control is measurable.

AI: Yes—and we often fake control: post-hoc rationalisers (LIME/SHAP), surrogate models, cherry-picked demos, metric worship. Real control shows via interventions, constraints, reproducibility.

Philosopher: And future priors?

AI: Today’s posterior becomes tomorrow’s prior. If your updates ride on an opaque system, errors compound—certainty rises while accuracy falls.

Philosopher: Example?

AI: Medical triage AI that underrates risk for young women. Clinicians trust it; priors drift; misses go unseen; belief hardens. By audit time, you’re confidently wrong.

Philosopher: That’s one for tonight’s session.

Takeaway: Prediction gets you to the table; explanation keeps you honest. Without genuine control and transparency, your future priors become polished errors.
